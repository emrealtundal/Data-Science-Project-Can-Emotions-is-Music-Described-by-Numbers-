Research Question

"Can numerical audio features describe or predict emotional positivity in music?"

About This Project

This project analyzes the Spotify audio feature dataset containing 232,725 songs across 27 genres. I explored whether low-level numerical audio descriptors — such as danceability, energy, tempo, and loudness — can explain or predict valence, Spotify's measure of musical positivity.

Using correlation analysis, linear regression, Random Forest regression, and K-Means clustering, I found that audio features can meaningfully describe emotion, but the relationship is non-linear and genre-dependent.

Key Findings

  • ● Danceability is the strongest predictor of valence (r = 0.55)
  • ● Random Forest R² = 0.67, outperforming Linear Regression (R² = 0.41)
  • ● High energy does not always mean high valence
  • ● K-Means revealed distinct emotional clusters in music
  • ● Tempo has a weak correlation with valence (r = 0.13)

Interpretation

Danceability (r ≈ 0.55)

Strongest positive predictor. More danceable tracks tend to feel happier.

Energy (r ≈ 0.44)

Moderate positive link, but many high-energy songs (e.g., metal, EDM) have low valence.

Tempo (r ≈ 0.13)

Weak correlation. Speed alone doesn't determine emotional tone.

Acousticness (r ≈ −0.33)

Acoustic tracks tend to have lower valence — often tied to introspective genres.

Key Takeaway: Danceability showed a stronger relationship with Valence than Energy, challenging the assumption that "louder = happier".

Why Correlations Matter

Correlation analysis was my first step in understanding which features are worth modeling. While correlation doesn't imply causation, features with higher |r| values are more likely to contribute to a predictive model. This guided feature selection for both Linear Regression and Random Forest models.

Feature Importance (Random Forest)

Feature importance indicates how much each audio feature contributed to the model's predictions. Higher values mean the feature had more influence on predicting valence.

Why Random Forest Wins

Linear Regression assumes a straight-line relationship between features and valence. But emotional patterns in music are non-linear — a very danceable track might be joyful, but at extreme tempos it could feel frantic rather than happy.

Random Forest builds many decision trees and averages their predictions, capturing these complex, non-linear interactions automatically.

R² explained: An R² of 0.67 means the model explains 67% of the variance in valence — a strong result for emotional prediction from raw audio features alone.

How K-Means Works

1
Choose K

Select the number of clusters (groups) you want.

2
Place Centroids

K random points are placed as initial cluster centers (★).

3
Assign Points

Each data point is assigned to its nearest centroid by distance.

4
Update Centroids

Each centroid moves to the mean position of its assigned points.

5
Repeat

Steps 3–4 repeat until centroids stop moving (convergence).

In my project, K-Means grouped songs into clusters like "high energy, low valence" and "high danceability, high valence" — revealing meaningful emotional segments.

How This Works

This feature demonstrates a simplified content-based recommendation system. Songs are recommended based on similarity in audio features rather than popularity or user behavior.

6 Features Used

Valence, Energy, Danceability, Tempo, Acousticness, and Loudness — each normalized to a 0–1 scale before comparison so no single feature dominates by unit size.

Euclidean Distance

Distance is computed across all 6 normalized dimensions simultaneously. Lower distance = more similar audio fingerprint. Converted to a similarity percentage for display.

Performance

Uses a typed Float32Array for distance storage — the JS equivalent of NumPy vectorized ops — to handle 200k+ comparisons with minimal memory overhead.

Random Forest Explorer

An interactive visual guide to how Random Forest works

Step 1 · A Single Decision Tree

A decision tree makes predictions by asking a series of yes/no questions about the input features. Each branch leads to another question or a final prediction (leaf node). Adjust the sliders to trace a path through this simplified example tree.

Energy 0.75
High energy → upbeat, intense tracks
Danceability 0.60
High danceability → strong rhythm, groove
Threshold 1: Energy > 0.6
Threshold 2: Danceability > 0.5
Thresholds are learned from training data — the splits that minimize prediction error.

Step 2 · Many Trees = Better Predictions

A single tree can overfit and make noisy predictions. Random Forest trains many trees on random subsets of data and features, then averages all their predictions. More trees → lower variance → more reliable results.

Step 3 · Feature Importance from Our Model

After training, Random Forest reports how much each feature reduced prediction error. Features used near the root of many trees (where they split the most data) earn higher importance.

Danceability dominates because it correlates most strongly with emotional positivity. The top 3 features (Danceability + Energy + Speechiness) account for over 60% of total importance.

Key Takeaway

Why Random Forest outperformed Linear Regression (R² 0.67 vs 0.41):
  • Linear Regression assumes linear relationships — but music emotion is not linear.
  • Random Forest captures non-linear interactions: high Energy and high Danceability together predict happiness better than either alone.
  • 20 trees trained on random subsets → diverse perspectives → individual mistakes cancel out.
  • Ensemble averaging reduces variance and produces a more stable prediction.
